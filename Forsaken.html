FORSAKEN

Anjorin · the Mindpiercer · an empath's unraveling

My earliest memory of my gift was helping my sister catch an old cockerel for slaughter. Other children cheered as we chased our supper, stumbling over each other — they rooted for the bird. What followed shamed me more than wetting myself.

I still remember the look on their faces — their cackles.

✦ ✦ ✦

I catch the bird and carry it to my sister, Olamide, its heart hammering against my palm.

She hands me a knife and says, "Bleed it."

"What?"

"Slit its throat and bleed it."

I've seen it done before, but I've never been asked to do it.

I pin the bird with my foot, crouch over it, and pluck the fine neck feathers to bare its skin for my blade.

A twinge shoots up my arm.

My grip loosens.

I drop the knife.

My heart thrashes inside me. I am the cockerel, moments from the blade.

I release it and recover from my daze. The laughter of the other children bursts around me like a storm.

I run for my mother's hut, my eyes welling with tears.

I hoped it would fade like a moonlit tale — bright for a night, gone by morning. But my story grew from that day. I became known as the boy who couldn't kill a chicken. A boy so tender-hearted it was bizarre. The adults shook their heads when I passed them. "Poor child," they would mutter. "He will never be a warrior."

"Sometimes, it is good to be afraid," my father says. "Wisdom starts with fear."

He lied. It's never a good thing to fear killing... a chicken.

The laughing stopped.

Then came the looks — longer, quieter. Heads tilted. Eyes soft.

I would have preferred the laughter.

When the oracles in my village discovered I was an empath, the pity stopped. For three moons, they kept me in seclusion. I ate alone and slept on a mat that smelled of herbs. They performed my itefa, reread my destiny. They gave me a new name: Anjorin. The old one vanished. I became an amoye too — a spiritual guide like them, a wise one. I was now a person people brought their problems to. Not someone expendable. Like a warrior.

"Forget he is younger," my mother would say to my cousins. "Take his advice."

Admiration and resentment followed me in equal measure.

Now that I'm grown, I'm the most popular empath in the kingdom, and still the most sensitive of creatures — still the boy who can't kill a chicken or a bug.

✦ ✦ ✦

In the middle of a session with a querent. I'm spiritually joined to her. I send her the warmth I've harvested from past experiences — mine and others'. She arches as if my hands rest on her. Her throat catches. Every sensation is real to her. Her jaw loosens, and she gasps. With no images to anchor it, she moves as though blindfolded.

Her fingers claw at the mat.

Her back arches, holds, and trembles, her voice breaking, "Please —" Not whole, not once.

I ease the Pleasurepain, as I call it, and she curls into herself, shuddering.

There's a dampness between my legs.

The wall against my bare back is cold.

My heartbeat steadies. My vision returns in fragments. A woman stands in the doorway of the sanctuary, mouth half-open.

My heart stutters.

Shola?

No, my love. It's not what you think.

She slips out the way she came.

I rush after her.

✦ ✦ ✦

Outside, the field rises and dips with the hills as Shola descends toward the village below. The wind lashes and swirls, stealing my breath until my chest caves. She cuts through my flock, past the sheep herds and the tents of my querents, and bolts across the pasture. After a stretch, she stoops over a ledge, panting.

"Stay back. Don't come any closer," she says.

"Fine. Please listen."

She claws at her braids and screams into the hills. I motion the gathering crowd away. I need a moment alone with my wife.

"I haven't been myself since I got back," I begin.

Birds screech overhead.

"I don't need touch to know what people feel — I sense it through the air."

Shola's face stills. Her knees buckle, and she sinks to the grass.

My words spill out. I explain how I became the most sought-after amoye in the land.

✦ ✦ ✦

When I finish, she stares into the hills beyond the village.

"Is that what you call it? Easing tensions?" she says finally, her voice flattening.

"I take away their pain. If only for a while. And in that moment... they're free. Isn't that what matters?"

"That woman had an orgasm."

"Spirit intrusion. I never touched her."

"It's intimacy. You share what should stay between us."

I don't respond.

She laughs once. "Those people don't want their problems solved; they come for you to satisfy their lust."

"They come to me because nothing else helps."

She shoots me a glance, her anger rising.

"You think I do it because I enjoy it?"

She sighs. "If you did nothing wrong, you wouldn't have kept it from me."

My silence answers. She knows I'm sorry.

"I need to be alone tonight. Don't come home tonight."

In the days that follow, I neither see nor hear from Shola. I cannot focus. I have cravings I no longer wish to indulge. Her words sour in me. Each time I swallow, they rise again — thick, refusing to go down.

I miss my sessions. Without them, the world dulls. Unfortunately, the bitter aftertaste of my hindsight hits me before I begin. Rather than deliver my querents the climax they seek, I describe it, hoping it dissuades: "At its peak, desire turns on itself — sweetness curdling into pain."

The days bleed into one another. I sleep on the same mat. Feel the same wind. Answer the same questions. I stay in the hills and refuse visitors. I listen to the wind. I hear the many questions my querents once asked me.

By the third day of my banishment, Shola's continued silence weighs like a judgment. I pick up the calabash I plan to surprise her with. Stop.

Set it down.

Pick it up again.

✦ ✦ ✦

On the fourth, a querent arrives with an unusual plea.

"My father is dying," she says. "He's in so much pain. Smallpox. Could you visit us and grant him a pleasant death?"

A punishment from Shoponna, the god whose name shall not be spoken. Her father must have violated a taboo. She is desperate, or she would not have confessed such news. "Where is he now?" I ask.

"In a forest north of Odàrà."

"Left to die?"

She nods, grimacing.

No one survives the contagion. With my gift, I could endure its ravages with less peril. An epidemic so absolute would feed my memory stores, making me more formidable.

The girl's name is Tade. She belongs to the Ọ̀runṣọlá Clan in Odàrà.

✦ ✦ ✦

That evening, Shola sends for me. When I arrive, my supper waits on a tray. She cuts off my attempt at conversation and goes straight to the point.

She watches me too long.

"Here's my decision," she says. "You have to choose between me and whatever you do in those hills."

"Then I choose you."

"That fast? You didn't even think about it."

"I don't need to. I love you, Shola."

"I love you too."

"There's a problem, though," I say. I roll a ball of eba, and dip it in soup and chunks of fish. "How to drive my followers away. They won't leave. So we will have to."

"Where will we go?"

"Odàrà. A new beginning. Time alone."

She leans her back against the wall. "I like how that sounds."

"We can leave at dawn."

"So soon? Why the urgency?"

"Why wait? Opportunity deserts those who hesitate."

"No more secrets, Anjorin."

"I swear it."

We make love.

It's the familiar telepathy of mood, our bodies twining with our inner chaos.

I breathe into her ear. "Let me show you what I can do now."

She peers into my eyes. She nods.

I introduce her to the Pleasurepain. I intend to show her just a little. But as soon as I open the channel, I spill over the cliff like a waterfall. I no longer steer it. I follow, attacking her sensibility with arousals I borrowed from harlots and princes — royal orgies.

She moans, her pleasure twisting into horror.

"Stop."

I can't.

I bare everything.

At last, I tear down the bridge, freeing us both. She stares at me, shaking. As if I've set the bed on fire.

I know what she thinks. I've inherited her reaction. I recognize them. I've seen them in first-time querents. I'm still searching for ways to free my partners of such thoughts without taking on their restraints.

"Was it that horrible?" I ask.

"Part of me..." She stops. Swallows. "Part of me wants to stay in it."

And that frightens her more than what I did.

"You play with things you don't understand," she says. "Things no oracle should."

I bite my tongue.

"Now I see why those who come to you won't leave."

After a while, she says, "Maybe their pain isn't yours to carry. Maybe the gods put it there for a reason. And you keep interrupting it."

I shrug. "All very possible."

"What if that's why we haven't been blessed with a child? We need to leave."

"I agree."

"And you need to purge yourself of all those..."

I nod. "So we leave in the morning?"

"Yes. How could I deny you the chance to start over?"

Shola sinks into her corner of the bed. She pushes me away when I wrap my arms around her. Once, we folded into each other without thinking, breaths braided in the dark. But tonight, we lie apart, a careful stretch of straw between us.

✦ ✦ ✦

Dawn arrives gray and reluctant. When I open my eyes, Shola is dressed. She sings softly as she stuffs garments into a pouch. I sit up, and she turns to me and smiles.

"There's water in the bowl over there," she says.

I wash my face in the bowl beside my altar, gather my cowries into a sack, lash it to a stick, and sling it over my shoulder. We hold hands and stride up the hill that blocks our view of the tableland. At the crest, she looks back at our home, and nostalgia brushes through her. Our neighbors will claim it if we don't return. But we will: I hid my earnings as 'the Mindpiercer' in a hole on that property.

I kiss her on the cheek and whisper in her ear, "If we leave all this behind... what do we have left?"

"A chance to start over?"

✦ ✦ ✦

We move along descending roads emptied of voices, following a familiar path to the outskirts, where wild vegetation begins. We no longer need to steady our gait with joined hands.

The air thickens with loam; shadows gather at the forest's edge. As sunlight thins among the trees, Shola reaches for my arm.

The sounds of human habitation peter out. The distant laughter, the clunking of axe against wood, and braying donkeys are replaced by a dense, listening quiet. The road narrows to uneven tracks; dust gives way to damp, clinging earth, and the smell of loam settles at the back of my throat. A cool breeze blows under tall, leafy canopies. My steps grow shorter and heavier. Shola winces as pain swells at her heel. It hurts. She shows me the blister on the ball of my foot. Each step is a small protest that she refuses to answer. A twig under my foot snaps and startles. Soon fear loosens its grip. Our gait develops a faster rhythm. Shadows close in, and the forest swallows us, the memory of sunlight and an open sky feeling like something we imagined.

She stops to listen out for danger. She presses a finger against her lips, and I stand still — even our breathing is too loud.

"Nothing will surprise us," I whisper. "I can sense spirits for miles, including the ones wearing bodies."

She relaxes. "What do you sense now?"

"Little creatures."

She frowns. "How can you tell?"

"They hunger for food or a mate. Nothing that should frighten us."

"And how will you know if that hunger isn't something large tracking us?"

I laugh. "I won't. Not always."

✦ ✦ ✦

By the time we reach Inúmìdún, the sun sinks, leaving a bruise of blue-violet across the sky. The market is closing: mats rolled, baskets overturned, goats dragged along as people hurry past.

"Why the rush?" Shola asks a goat seller, who knots her tether tight.

"You must be a visitor," the woman says, eyes flicking down the road.

Shola nods. "We've come from Irawo."

"Tonight is Òrò."

"I see. Is there an inn nearby?"

The woman studies us, then nods toward a narrowing street. "Come."

She leads us, her three goats pulling at their ropes, to an ilé àlejò.

The place reeks of burúkútù and men who have drunk past sense. Their laughter is a dull blade; their eyes linger too long on my wife. I draw her closer.

There is no choice. On a night like this, no woman should be outside when Òrò begins. I press two cowries into the keeper's palm. He says nothing, only leads us to our hut.

✦ ✦ ✦

Night settles by the time we step into the yard to bathe. Shola lathers herself while I keep watch beside the bath shed. Nearby, two men share a keg of palm wine.

"The festival could not have come at a better time. These are troubling times."

"It's that mind-piercer in Irawo many go to see."

"He indulges immorality. That's why the Òrò must be called."

Fools. I pull thorns from broken spirits. It lasts only a moment, but sometimes a moment is all that's needed to change a life.

We return to our hut when Shola emerges. She slips off her wrapper and dries her hair with it, slow and deliberate.

"I would give anything to sit by a warm fire."

Outside, the bullroarer bellows.

"Do you hear that? It's the festival. I want to experience it."

"You can't go out there — you're an outsider."

"My body will remain here."

She hesitates. "Anjorin, please. Don't leave."

"If anything frightens you, touch me, and I will sense your panic. But don't alarm me without cause."

I lie on my side and step outside her protests, extending beyond her worry into the emotional landscape.

Anger streams past — the Àṣẹ of the ancestors — restless, agitated, swirling around the bullroarer. They mark their presence through footpaths — a surge of wrath, then a wash of sorrow.

The bullroarer bellows again — closer.

The sound presses against the air, heavy, waiting.

I feel something I cannot make out.

✦ ✦ ✦

I wake with a wince. Light pierces my eyes. Morning. The sun warms the air. Shola squeezes a loincloth into her sack.

"We should have left before sunrise," I say, shielding my face. "We will need to cover our faces now."

"I think we should see a priest."

"A priest? Why?"

She stares. "You remember nothing from last night?"

She tells me how I awoke, choking on tears, my voice shaking as I spoke of going to Odàrà to soothe the anguish of a godforsaken. To harvest the punishment of one abandoned and fold it into my reserves. She tells me how I wrestled aloud with whether it would be mercy or blasphemy.

I'm conscious of my breathing. What in Eshu's name loosened my tongue? Now she knows everything.

"What will a priest do, besides pray?"

"A message from the gods, perhaps. Something you can both accept."

I exhale. "I will not see a priest."

She folds the cloth into her sack and turns to me. "Then we part here. You go your way; I go mine."

"You're serious."

"I said no more secrets, and you lied." She meets my gaze. "Someone needs to save you from yourself."

"Fine. Let's go."

We throw cowls over our heads, pull the cloth across our mouths, and slip into the street. A slow tide of worshippers carries us along between hamlets.

✦ ✦ ✦

At a pantheon, incense hangs thick. A drum pulses beneath murmured confessions. After the festival, the place swells with penitents and people ready to shed old skins.

During prayer, I lean toward Shola. "My stomach growls."

"Sssh. Pray. Offer your fasting."

When the prayers end, she pays for a private consultation with the priest who led the session — a man of a lower calling than me.

We wait alone in a shrine.

I lean toward her. "I am an amoye. His training could never match my calling."

"Humble yourself. If darkness sits inside you, isn't it better that he cannot see it clearly?"

Good foresight. But she's forgetting one thing: mediums like me can shape these moments.

The priest shuffles in, ankle beads clinking. We hold our greetings as he lowers himself onto a mat, face tightening with effort. Not old — heavy. Flesh burdens him more than years.

"So what answers do you seek?"

"We are on our way to Odàrà," Shola says, "to grant a dying man a swift, painless death. What lies in store for us?"

He works the Ifá Oracle, chanting as he summons Òrúnmìlà. When the sign falls, he gathers Shola's hands. "You will triumph."

He turns to repeat the rite for me.

"Don't touch him. He was recently treated for a contagion. We are not sure if it has passed."

The priest presses his hands together.

He peers into my eyes, gaze clouding. "A man who fears nothing has no boundaries" — Shola's heart pounds — "Suffering is often needed for repentance. Mercy isn't always the road that reunites you with your manifest destiny..."

I slip into him before he finishes. I lend clarity to his words.

"Fear is often an obstacle. But you have overcome yours. Go with the gods."

I sever the link. The priest blinks awake.

Shola's heart relaxes.

We leave the shrine before the priest fully recovers.

✦ ✦ ✦

We eat in silence at a stall near the pantheon's gate. By the time the sun climbs higher, Shola is humming and breaking yams with her fingers. With palm oil gleaming on her lips, she's a different woman from the one who recounted my words.

I place my hand over hers. She does not pull away. "You don't seem to have reservations about Odàrà."

She shrugs. "You're mistaken about the man. He's sick, not godforsaken. The priest told you to go with the gods."

I force a smile. "What if he has smallpox?"

Her fingers still. "Then the gods want his punishment eased."

"Including the One Who Shall Not Be Named."

She sighs. "Yes."

It's a mistake to touch her. She tastes my guilt. She knows I'm hiding something.

"I never feared losing you. I thought if I needed to know whether you loved me, I would only need to touch you. But now... you can send emotions that aren't yours. I don't know you anymore. That frightens me."

"Oracles must grow to fulfill their purpose."

"I worry your path won't include me."

I pull her close and press a kiss to her forehead.

✦ ✦ ✦

When we finish eating, the sun climbs high, casting broken light through the canopy. The village loosens its hold; hamlets spread farther apart.

Ahead, the forest beckons.

Under the shade of leafy canopies, gnarled roots bulge from the soil like knuckled hands. Dry leaves crack underfoot.

We pick our way through the forest, Shola close, her shoulder brushing mine. Tension hums through her. I trade my calm for her state of mind and startle at once — the bushes rasp too loudly, each sound pricking her nerves.

Off the path, a river hurtles downhill, its black-and-silver skin splitting into restless seams. Shola edges toward it, soles testing slick earth. We fill our gourds.

A prickle runs along my spine.

"Let's get out of the water," I say. "Something's hiding beneath."

She freezes, eyes locked on the shifting current.

I reach into the water. A crocodile erupts, thrashing.

Shola runs up the bank and doesn't stop until her lungs burn.

I release the crocodile, and it swims downstream.

"The gods be praised," Shola coos, making her way back a step at a time.

Something resting in the trees pricks up.

A pulse. Distant. Hungry.

I withdraw my spirit force. Too late. The damage is done. Whatever-it-is sensed the Pleasurepain and is now aware of us.

Shola's hand finds mine. She's shaking from almost being caught by a crocodile. We have bigger worries.

"We need to move," I say. "Now."
