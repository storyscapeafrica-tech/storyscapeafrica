Part One: The Propagandist

She’s at a coffeehouse for a secret meeting with an informant who promised to reveal Zee-Zee’s location for a price. The informant, who introduces himself as Jamal, reaches into his thobe and pulls out a badge.
C-I-A.

A guitar flicks in her ear, a musical sting. The banjo player’s timing rattles her. Busking between tables, he picks the moment she realizes she’s been played to strum a short, needling solo.

The banjo player leaves, and her thoughts flood in.

So he’s CIA? But why the deception? Why lure her to a coffeehouse on the south side of Damascus? What’s their angle? To gauge what she knows, then hunt Zee-Zee themselves? What could they possibly want with her?

It’s 9:15 p.m. No women in the coffeehouse. Too late for respectable women to be out. Husbands and fathers would disapprove. As a tourist, she can bend a few rules, but even though she craves a smoke, she won’t risk it.

Groups of men in keffiyehs eat and laugh, sputtering as they talk with full mouths.

She turns to the man at her table.

What’s he saying?

The sight of his badge put her in a time capsule; she missed everything he said after that, focusing on the banjo’s fading rhythm, the soft clatter of dishes in the kitchen, and the chatter of guests. The sounds—chatter, dishes, rhythm—fuse into a haunting musical score.
