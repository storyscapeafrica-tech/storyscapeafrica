THE SEED

Egba, Oyo Kingdom · 1797 · an empath's burden

I pin the boy to the wall, stare him down, and lean in. His eyes bulge until his pupils are islands in a sea of white.

"What more harm do you plan to do?" I growl.

He stares. His fear pulses and chimes. There is another presence.

Olurebi pushes me aside and shakes the boy roughly.

"Speak," he snaps. "Besides miscarriages, what else?"

The boy's lower lip curls inward. He wails.

"I will not ask again," Olurebi snarls.

"Iya, ṣe iranlọwọ fun mi." The boy calls for his mother.

A small crowd gathers behind us. No one intervenes.

"I didn't do anything," he mutters.

We press him with questions to confirm what we already know: he does not know what he is — does not know he has an indwelling.

Olurebi turns to the crowd. "This boy is why many of you keep losing your pregnancies."

The crowd gasps.

"Ademola? So this is what you have been doing?" an elderly man says.

"I didn't do anything. I swear."

I shove the boy into the crowd, and his kinsmen crack blows against his skull — solid, bone-deep thuds. He covers his head with his arms and crouches. "Iya mi-o!" he pleads.

They pry him loose, strike his bare back with their palms. The boy runs and hugs his mother. Voices yell at her — "Hit him." She pushes him away and brandishes her soup ladle — "Get back."

"Knock his head with it," they shout.

The metal connects.

The boy staggers back, eyes wide with shock. He tries to flee, but they catch him and haul him back.

I am an empath.

"Can you see?" I say to him. "Your mother does not want you. Your father does not want you. Nobody wants you. You are no longer welcome here. So you must come with us. We will take you where your kind is accepted."

I extend my hand to the weeping child, and he places his fingers in my palm.

As we lead him away, no one bids farewell — only eyes set in frowning faces.

Even after years of the same routine, it does not get easier. The pain of rejection I have witnessed will haunt me for days. I wish I were a seer like Olurebi. Or a clairaudient. Or an augur — anything but what I am.

The day is still young, and we have a journey ahead. The boy will stop crying at some point, but I will carry his heartbreak for much longer.

✦ ✦ ✦

We travel through whispering woods, the leaves speaking in low, ceaseless tongues as they brush against one another. Now and again, we stop — not to rest, but to work — setting crude traps for small prey, fingers moving by habit, eyes scanning the undergrowth for signs of movement. The day stretches above. We measure it by our shadows — short at first, then drawn thin across the forest floor. We walked so long that each step borrowed from the last — instinct over relaxation. If I had been a pregnant woman, I might have lost the child by now.

The boy feels it too. I see it in the careful way he places his feet, in the silence he wears like a second skin. I try to break it. I speak to him — small, passing things — and slowly, cautiously, he answers. His voice is thin at first, but it steadies.

His name is Ademola. He is twelve.

"Where are we going?" he asks at last.

"We are taking you to Ile-Jade."

He walks a few paces more before speaking again. "Will they punish me there?"

"No one will harm you. You will have a new family. You will meet other children — children like you."

"I do not want a new family."

His words settle between, heavier than the air.

"No other place will you be welcome. Ile-Jade is different. The wind there carries a pollen that loosens curses. It is a place where children like you cannot harm anyone."

"Children like me?"

"Curse carriers."

We walk on.

Through the thinning trees, our destination begins to gather itself in the distance — not a structure, not a clearing, but something that holds its own presence. Ile-Jade.

Birds startle at our approach, lifting in a rush of wings before settling again on higher branches, watching.

This is where the boy will remain. Here he will learn the forest's quieter language — how to gather herbs without bruising them, how to read the soil, how to carry what others cannot. The alchemists will take him in, shape his days, give him a place within the rhythm of this hidden world. Whatever he becomes must happen here, beneath this forest cathedral.

In Ile-Jade, he will grow, find a girl, and father children. And one day, he will return to the same earth that now bears his steps.

✦ ✦ ✦

The next day, Olurebi and I journey back.

We part ways at Ekpe Creek.

I follow the road that leads to Egba land.

It is market day in my village. I run into traders carrying farm produce — baskets bowed with yams, gourds slick with morning dew, bundles of leafy greens tied with raffia that bite into their palms. I help them where I can, the weight settling into my shoulders, dust rising around our feet. At the market square, they shower me with blessings and make their way to their stalls, dissolving into the widening hum of voices, the bright scatter of color, the restless, breathing body of the crowd.

Shola stands half-turned to a fishmonger, a clay pot braced against the curve of her hip as though grafted to her. She laughs unguardedly at something the seller says.

As I approach, our eyes meet.

For a moment, the crowd noise slips into the background. She startles as recognition flares — it breaks across her face like sudden panic. She is not just beautiful; she is luminous in a way that gladdens me.

She hands the fishmonger her clay pot and comes toward me, weaving through bodies, brushing past shoulders and baskets, unmindful of the protests that rise in her wake.

The space between collapses. She loops her arms around my neck and draws me in, fitting herself against me as if to test that I am still whole. Her head settles into the hollow of my shoulder; her breath warms my skin. Relief moves through her in small, steady waves, threaded with something deeper.

I hold her just as tightly.

I am home.

Oh, how I have missed her.

Words falter. Our bodies speak enough.

I do not deserve her. Girls like Shola are sought after — claimed by warriors whose bodies promise safety, whose strength glints like iron in the sun. I am the opposite.

Shola is different. She listens for the mind, the heart, the quiet voice beneath the noise.

Fatigue finds me in her arms, sudden and heavy. My knees buckle, and she catches me — she slips my arm over her shoulder and steadies me. She guides me toward the fishmonger's shade.

"Baba, please bring us a mat," she says.

I do little that day beyond eating my mother's food and sleeping.

Shola comes and goes.

When evening leans in, she rises for the last time.

"I will see you tomorrow," she says.

✦ ✦ ✦

At first light the following day, I am back on the farm with my clan, ridging the earth, pressing seeds into its damp breath.

"Look," Tolani says. "Is that not Shola?"

I turn. A lean figure approaches along the narrow path.

It is her — she has slipped away from her chores again.

She greets everyone as she comes, laughter threading through the morning, but her eyes keep returning to mine.

✦ ✦ ✦

When it's time to rest, Shola and I sit together and play Àkòdà, our fingers tracing patterns into the soft soil. Tolani and the others pass around a gourd of kparaga. I never touch it — sharp, bitter as kola, a farmer's fire.

Tolani knows this. Still, he extends the gourd, that crooked grin tugging at his mouth.

"Anjorin, come on. Just a small one."

"No, thank you."

"Water revives you. This will give you strength."

"I said no."

We return to our game. The air hangs heavy, damp with earth and the sense that Tolani is not finished.

"Do not bother," he says. "Strong drink is for men with iron stomachs."

"Please ignore him," Shola murmurs.

Tolani laughs, louder now. "Forgive me, Anjorin. I forget — you have no use for strength. Everyone excuses your weakness because you are an amoye. Even Shola will excuse your limp cock."

The grove tightens. Leaves hold still. Even the birds sharpen their calls.

Shola's fingers bite into my arm, trying to anchor me.

A mistake.

The instant our skin meets, something sparks — dry kindling catching flame. Her steadiness slips; my bitterness floods in.

She rises, closing the space between her and Tolani. "Are you drunk? Do not ever speak like that again."

He smirks.

It lasts a breath too long.

Her fist cracks against his jaw.

He staggers, stunned. She moves again, but he catches her braids and yanks. Pain folds her. He strikes — once, twice.

She collapses.

One moment she is on the ground; the next, I have Tolani in a chokehold, his pulse hammering under my arm. The world narrows — blood, breath, the dark pull of something rising.

He claws at my wrists. I drive my knee into his ribs. He throws his weight forward, but I do not release him. We crash. I tighten.

Something shifts.

His fury spills into me — jealousy, sharp and metallic, filling my mouth, flooding my chest. Heat builds, unbearable.

I turn it back on him — tighten, hold.

I do not want to kill him. But the longer we touch, the more his impulse takes root, bending my will. The world contracts to pressure and air. My vision dims.

Shola's rejection burns — his rejection, now mine. I taste it, raw and poisonous. I want her gone. I want —

Tolani weakens. His body slackens. The heat breaks.

I come back.

The others are watching — waiting to see if I will finish it.

I shove him away.

He collapses onto the grass, gasping.

The connection snaps. The world widens.

I look at him — at what I felt through him — and something hard settles.

"If you touch her again," I say, voice rough and certain, "I will kill you."

It is not a threat. It is a fact I now understand.

It is the first time I taste that darkness. It will not be the last.

As an empath who relies on touch to understand others, all my lovers would have been my soulmates.

But there has been only one: Shola.

She has stood by me through everything. Whenever our emotional currents merge, the line between loving her and loving myself dissolves. Her feelings anchor my sense of being and vice versa; I am her and she is me. It is like finding a stable state beyond sexual climax. It is not something you fully grasp until you have lived it: two lovers, two separate pulses, distinct and deliberate, our boundaries dissolving into rhythm, sensation finding symmetry, growing in centering as breath folds into breath and bodies move as one — one heartbeat, one wave of pleasure.

Marrying her was the finest decision I ever made: a vow that kept me tethered to a vital organ, the other half of my living pulse.

✦ ✦ ✦

On our wedding night, nothing unfolded as we planned. It became the night that changed everything.

Shola and I breathe in tandem, caught in the arc of our own fire, our bodies swaying to a rhythm older than memory. Heat builds, electric and familiar. My gift, wild and ungovernable, stirs beneath my skin; she knows what is coming — the sweet inversion, the merging, the surrender that erases where I end and she begins. Muscles tighten. Our cries coil into each other, a current of sound and sensation that blurs the border of flesh and thought. The harder I move, the louder we become — or perhaps our voices drive the motion, a loop of mutual ignition.

A breeze parts the straw mat veiling our doorway and glides within. Sly, alive, it teases the calabashes and stirs the masks along the wall. The incense vessel flares, its fragrance unfurling, rich and unrelenting. Amber tongues of flame lick the wooden faces hanging above. I take this strange wind as nature's rarity, a pulse of àṣẹ awakening the limen between worlds.

Shola stiffens — the spell fractures.

She jerks upright, eyes wide. A cold touch grazes my shoulder — a hand belonging to neither. She felt it first. Her scream tears through the air, followed by the dull thud of her body hitting the floor.

A shadow stands over me — dense, human. The figure leans in — thick arms, heavy breath — one hand clamping over my mouth, the other pressing me down into the straw. The night, once warm and sacred, collapses into silence and fear.

"Be quiet," he grates in my ear. "I will not harm you."

Through our contact, I feel his calm — no trace of the anger that breeds violence. He is telling the truth.

"Tell her to stop," he whispers gruffly, his breath against my nape.

"Shola, stop screaming," I say.

Shola's piercing scream gives way to rapid breathing.

The wind fades.

Shola turns up the lantern, quivering. "Who are you? What do you want?" she says to the intruder.

"Assistance," he says, getting off my back. "We need your help, Anjorin."

"You cannot come in like this and expect me to—"

"Can we talk privately?"

I hesitate. "Wait for me outside. As you can see, I am not dressed. Neither is my wife."

The intruder leaves.

"Do not go," Shola begs.

I tie my loincloth around my waist, search for my dagger, find it next to the fishing spears I was sharpening. I place it in my loincloth's scabbard and cover everything with a straw kilt.

"Relax. I will be back. Please stay inside," I say and push past the stiff bamboo curtain.

The evening fire sputters, coughing out its last breaths. Frail tongues of flame lick at the night air, throwing shadows across the bodies of four intruders — faces streaked with black paint, their eyes shining ghost-white through the darkness. They hover in the half-lit courtyard, the glow sketching their shaved heads and the metal glint of their blades. Around them, the huts stand like mute sentinels, circling the courtyard in uneasy sleep.

"Ajasin is my name," the intruder says, "and these are my men. We are the Afefe."

The Afefe?

I fall to my knees, honored to be in their presence.

"Get up," Ajasin croaks. "There is no time. Are you ready to serve a higher calling?"

"I am."

"Come with us."

"But my wife—"

"She will not want you to leave with us, and if she tries to raise the alarm, we may be forced to silence her permanently."

His words hang, blunt and cold — not a threat, but just as distressing. A primal pull overrides my resistance. I fall behind the men, my feet propelling me like a young zebra against its mother. The six of us slip past the faint echoes of night drunks, our footfalls heard only by owls. I lose my bearings in the blindfold of darkness and inner turmoil. These strangers seem more familiar with the terrain than I am.

✦ ✦ ✦

Dawn breaks, pale and hesitant. The sharp, smoky scent of roasted pig fills the air. Around a crackling fire, five strange men sit on logs, their postures relaxed. My roof is gone, replaced by open sky and the faint hum of wilderness. Panic flickers in my chest — where am I?

Memories of last night rush back: the shadowy figure in my bedroom, the four others outside.

We are camped beside a restless river. Herdsmen drive their flocks into the shallows; villagers wade in to bathe and fill their gourds. Children watch us from afar, silent, unblinking.

A pot-bellied man breaks from the bank and ambles toward us.

"O guardians of the graves, mighty priests of the Oracle, voice of the gods," he says, bowing slightly. "A matter troubles my village — one that has confounded even our wisest men."

"Not now. Later." One of the men from last night flicks a hand in dismissal.

The man hesitates, then withdraws.

My hosts are the Afefe. Isn't that what they said?

The name is not unfamiliar. I learned it as an amoye: a secretive order bound to sacred work, moving in shadows, carrying out the will of Oya — the goddess of rebirth — ensuring no soul finds its way back into flesh without her consent.

And now, for reasons I am yet to understand, they need my help.

"Ah. You are awake," Ajasin's voice cuts through the haze. "Come join us."

Their veined arms end in hands that seem molded from living clay. Ajasin — the bearded one — bears a chest like carved stone and a neck coiled thick and strong, a python of muscle beneath his skin.

I stumble forward, my legs shaky, sleep clouding my vision. I shuffle onto a log, but before I can regain my balance, one of them splashes me with a bowl of water, soaking my straw kilt. The icy sensation impales me.

Their laughter swells.

"Here. Smell this," another says, thrusting a gourd under my nose and uncapping it. An acrid stench fills my nostrils, clawing into my lungs. My body recoils, and I pass out.

I wake on the ground, their cackles rising — a chorus of merciless amusement.

"That should wake him," Ajasin says.

He introduces his amused men. I do not pay attention. The only thing that registers is that they have such outdated names.

"We need your help to capture 'The Seed,'" Ajasin says. "It is an ajogun."

"Why me?"

"You are an empath. The ajogun we seek carries a curse that overwhelms minds, forcing them to feel what they should not. We need someone who can tell whose mind has not been possessed — someone who can trace the obsession back to its source."

I shake my head. "I cannot feel anything without first making physical contact."

"That is because you have not been properly trained. No disrespect to your olukos, but they seem to believe in waiting for Orunmila's appointed time. You waste years praying when meditation would serve better."

That is not accurate. We pray to the gods to direct us because it is just as easy to receive guidance from demons. Some doors should not be opened without knocking first. It could mean losing your purpose — your destiny. This is why we pray.

"We will teach you to harness your gift. When we are done, you will sense emotions without touch."

"Why is the Afefe after an ajogun? You are usually concerned with bigger things."

"Ajoguns are supposed to change fortunes, not people. Things return to normal when they are taken away. But this one changes people. Changing people means changing their destinies — their ayanmo. They will not be allowed to reincarnate when they die. This is why we need to get rid of it."

A question hangs in my silence.

"The spirit of sexual unrestraint," Ajasin says, as if he read my mind. "It bewitches them with it. A man leaves his wife's bed and becomes a slave to his desires, and is ready to die for it. Sex is an ìrírí that both binds and blinds. Even incest."

I never imagined it possible: a situation where a community would be unwilling to give up its ajogun. Unthinkable as this may be, the Seed, as they call it, is a cherished hexer.

I spend whole seasons shaping my gift, sanding it down to precision.

The first time, I reach outward, nothing happens.

The second, something bites back.

I close my eyes, open a channel, but miss when I try to sink into a hawk's being. So they catch one for me and hold it close. I send my life force outward and into it. Its fear makes my bladder leak. Its hunger gnaws in my gut as if it were my own.

By my third attempt, I am free. I am free but as heavy as a cloud. I'm inclined to things that could never interest me, like eating tiny chicks. I raid chicken coops and carry them off.

What bewilders me most is my strange desires. How can I crave the closeness of honey badgers, bush babies, and porcupines as if they were Shola?

✦ ✦ ✦

One evening, Ajasin and his men return from the forest, a wild pig thrashing in their grip — its hooves kick at the air, its breath ragged and defiant. They tether it to the ground and spark a fire, the smell of kindling rising, an omen in smoke. Knives whisper against stone. When the blade finally kisses flesh, the scream that follows shreds the night.

I cannot stand it. The sound burrows into me, tearing at the thin membrane between sanity and chaos. I trade emotions with the creature to calm it. Its terror floods my chest, sharp and blinding; its pain blooms in every nerve. It is unbearable. And then nothing.

When I surface, Ajasin's face looms above, eyes wide, mouth a blade of sound. He is shouting, his voice hard enough to drag me out of the dark.

"Do not ever do that again, you hear me? You could have gotten yourself killed. All our efforts would have been in vain."

"What were you thinking?" Adenikiyen chimes in.

"Your mind is both your strength and your weakness," Ajasin says. "Something like that could stop your heart. Shock can kill."

At night, under the dim flicker of the campfire, they eat in silence. I sit apart on a rock, far from their fire and farther from them. I cannot bear to look at their meal; I felt its torment, its despair, and it was more than I could stomach. Badimun and Adenikiyen — Adeniki, as everyone calls him — retell the story. They say my scream ripped through the forest like an animal's dying cry, raw and unbearable. That is how they knew. Jibowu reached me first and struck me unconscious, saving me from permanent madness or even death.

But what they are missing is why I did it. They have never been inside a head like mine. Never felt another's pulse as their own. My empathic shifts once required the simplest bridge — skin against skin — but now they need only my will. With this new reach, the air itself carries another's ache into me. The murder of the pig hollowed me out. I wanted release, not from life, but from the helplessness of watching pain. I traded my calm for the animal's terror to lift its suffering, even if it ended mine.

They don't know the full extent of my gift, how it allows my targets to switch places with me.

How something stronger than intuition draws me in.

How Joy comes like rain — sudden, loud. Gone.

How Sorrow sits with me.

And learns my name.

And I forget how to be me.

I have not been myself for some time.

Something inside has shifted, something essential. Whenever I return from the depths of a strange emotion, I pretend to be the same man. But the truth is more complex: I am changed. And so are they. For them, it is a temporary crossing; for me, a repeating unraveling that leaves me more unstitched. Now that distance is no longer a barrier and physical contact is no longer essential, I must unlearn how to penetrate the unconscious state.

My hosts live by the rhythm of earth and flame. They hunt what the forest offers, fell trees with sure hands, and split the trunks into neat cords of firewood. Smoke, sweat, and the scent of roasting meat fill the air. My role is to sleep, to listen, and to learn. I train every day, sometimes without supervision. Badimun teaches me to be steadfast in my vicariousness and not cringe.

The days here drag like a dwindling flame — like our morning fires, flaring at dawn and fading by nightfall. But unlike them, it brings no warmth, no solace. Loneliness clings like smoke.

I miss home. I miss the palm trees — the way they whisper and bow as I pass. I miss how, from a distance, hamlets huddle together. I miss how mine stands apart, its vast roof a mountain of straw. I miss the pale eaves that spill from it like a frozen waterfall. I miss the dimness of the narrow alley that guides me to the glow in the central courtyard. Shola is kneeling in the yard, braiding a child's hair. Her smile unfolds as slowly as sunrise when she looks up and sees me.

Shola. I miss my dearest Shola.

I think of her constantly and know she is doing the same. My long absence could drive her to despair — cost her her life, if it has not already. This fear gnaws. How can I explain this to men who cannot imagine what it means to live without a wife, as their vows forbid marriage? They may have known the flesh of a woman, but certainly not the communion Shola and I share: that living dialogue of breath and heartbeat, the sacred recognition when two emotions fuse into one current. They do not know what it is to lose that, to wake in silence instead of with a heart that beats with yours.

"You are distracted," Badimun yells. "Focus, Anjorin. This is where you need to be."

He is shaping my mind, toughening it. Not for telepathy, but for the task at hand. Each lesson is both a new territory and captivity. Besides teaching me how to reach into a creature's agitation without touch, they teach me to think like them. They want total obedience. But they want me to understand why I should give it.

Ajasin oversees it all. He keeps me close by taking me to far-off places — beyond the echo of voices — where it is the two of us.

He has taken me to mountains close to Aganju's throne, where rainforests pulse beneath like living tapestries. Air currents howl around, carrying the voice of the wild. Deep below, hidden from sight, a river murmurs. There, where nature is ancient and immense, he would discuss our mission — its sacredness — rehashing his worldview: "We must protect a mortal's ayanmo — his disposition for self-improvement, which is the only prerequisite for reincarnation." He tries to bend my mind, like the canopies shifting and shimmering in the wind.

On one such jaunt, I ask him the questions I have been holding back:

"Why can I not practice on humans?"

"Do not worry about that. If it works on one creature, it works on all."

"I cannot tell what you are feeling. Why?"

"The gods protect us from the occult. Only your touch can penetrate us."

Gingerly, I reach for his shoulder, and instantly, a cyclone rises.

"Touch me, and I will break your arm," he says.

I pull back my hand.

The cyclone fades.

"Why do you refer to the Seed as 'it'?"

"Because we do not know if it is male or female."

The more we speak, the clearer it becomes that the jinx is no longer a child, considering how long it has stayed hidden. An adult ajogun? Fascinating. I have never encountered one. Is there any chance it is not aware of its witchery? I doubt it.

"This is what we know," Ajasin says. "We know where it resides. We know that the chief of its village has weaponized it."

"How?"

"The chief invites the royals of other villages to secret orgies. After such gatherings, members of his court marry the visiting royals, binding their villages to his influence."

"Clever."

"Your role will be to identify the Seed. We will wipe out everyone else once we have it."

"Wipe out? Is that not a bit extreme?"

"It is necessary. They are too far gone. If we send it to Ile-Jade, they will follow it there. Surely you understand this."

I nod.

"Do not worry. We will whisk you away before we begin."

His facial expressions reveal nothing, his emotions inscrutable. I cannot tell if he is struggling with what he must do. If the wind reflects his inner turmoil, then he is calm.

I test my theory: "Is genocide the only solution? The logical answer would be to kill the Seed. But of course, we know that killing an ajogun is taboo. They are protected by the very curses they carry. Ending the life of one would unleash an intergenerational plague on its killers and their clans. I think the gods have given us a puzzle to solve. Let us not rush—"

The wind squalls. Trees convulse.

"There is no puzzle. This is what needs to be done. Unholy alliances lead to moral compromises. If we do not act now, they will end up in Orun Apadi when they die. Better for them to die now than when their souls will not be allowed back."

"But—"

"Enough. You human oracles think you know more than those who wield the Ifa oracle handed down by Orunmila himself."

"I am sorry."

The wind abates.

"I hope I have not made a mistake selecting you."

"You could not have. I believe Orunmila is guiding you."

No one questions the Afefe. Their missions are divine. Even when we do not understand them, we must believe they serve the greater good, even if they involve genocide. Gift or curse; good or evil. No absolutes. The circumstance changes everything.

"Tomorrow," Ajasin says, breaking the silence, "we will set out for the place where the next orgy is to take place. You are ready."

"Thank you."

Not only has my training given me greater control over my gift, but it has taught me how to save emotional states for later use. I keep the pig's terror in a place where I can recall it.

That night, stretched out on my mat beneath the low hum of the forest, I watch an owl settle on a branch — a pale sentinel against the dark. Its stillness calls. I draw my focus inward, then outward, until my awareness threads itself through the shape of its shadow. Slowly, my mind drifts into its orbit. Our thoughts align, two pulses folding into one quiet rhythm. I feel the bird ease, its breath softening, while mine sharpens with clarity. Every sound deepens, every scent unfurls; I am awake inside another body.

Then I open the vault within — the one brimming with old, caged emotion — and let it surge. Grief, terror, the memory of death spill through the link, flooding the creature's small heart with the final convulsions of a greater beast. The shock courses through its nerves like fire through dry grass. A tremor, a stillness — then silence.

The owl tumbles from its perch without a sound, heavy as fruit in the night air, dead before the earth can catch it.

My heart races with awe and dread. It is what I expected: I can weaponize my gift.
