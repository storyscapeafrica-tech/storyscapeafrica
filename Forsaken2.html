FORSAKEN

Part 2 · the forest of egberes · five buried, one bound

With the sun at its peak, we rest on the banks. Shola's hand drifts to my arm, a teasing brush. The contact flips our states — her feelings flood me, mine spill into her.

She jerks back, eyes widening.

"You're tense. Something bothers you."

There's a mystic force in the woods. And it's aware of me. I don't want to alarm her so I smile.

"Relax," she says. "You're the gods' chosen. You have their protection."

"What if you are right? What if there's darkness in me? I've felt the darkness of others..."

"Sssh. Don't speak like that. You have a purpose." She glances over my shoulder and flinches.

I turn.

"I saw someone." She points toward a pocket of shadow.

A hunter stumbles through brush and vanishes behind palm fronds, lean, ribs shifting beneath taut skin. He lowers his face to bark and roots, dragging his nose along them.

"He's starving," Shola says.

Unease winds like a python around me. I should have sensed him as a man. But I sensed no human presence.

The hunter crawls into the bushes.

"That man isn't what he seems." I spring forward into the undergrowth.

Shola runs after me. "Anjorin!"

"I need to know what it is."

I crash through brush taller than my shoulders. Roots snag my feet.

Where did it go?

Shapes ahead resolve into the hunter, then collapse back into stumps.

I've lost it.

Shola stumbles across roots to get to me. "What madness came over you?"

I peer behind fronds. "It vanished somewhere."

"Are you even listening?"

"Woman. This 'madness' is what I am. I keep things from you because you refuse to see me."

She stares, wide-eyed, silent.

A shadow shifts ahead.

The hunter.

I follow.

Branches close behind him without sound.

The path disappears.

My foot catches —

The ground tilts.

Something moves above me.

I look up —

Teeth.

Darkness.

The hunter hangs in the branches, eyes bulging with a strangled stare.

It opens its mouth, and something sears through me.

My vision tears.

Then it holds.

✦ ✦ ✦

I float between branches, looking down as my body hangs limp in the hunter's arms. My head lolls, chest rising.

I am outside it. Watching.

I'm in a strange limbo devoid of emotions.

I could stay like this forever. I'm neither happy nor sad — no inner drive. Nothing concerns me, and I care about nothi...

Òlórun. What inhabits my body?

Shola kneels beside my slack form. "Anjorin."

I strike it —

not with my hands, but with the force of a link.

I reach for it.

And it reaches back.

I care. I care about getting back to my body.

The connection catches.

For a moment, we share one space.

I wrench.

I rip it from my body.

It howls.

Kill it, Anjorin. It's evil.

The voice grows. Not a thought. A reflex. Mindless, ancient, patient — the way a river rises and floods. The way rot finds the soft part of fruit.

I am hollow. Like the space between the ribs of a skeleton.

No grief. No fear. No love.

I won't mourn it.

Go on. End its existence in the physical world.

No. Stop.

It's mischief, not malice. A lonely spirit reaching — longing to be human.

In a heartbeat, I'm back.

My empathy is back.

Pain tears through me, and I release it.

The vagabond spirit slips back into my body.

As with the cockerel, its will to live surges into me. I despair. I fight, then yield. Once again, I cannot hold what I am meant to kill.

"Anjorin." Shola pounds my chest.

Her touch opens a path.

No.

✦ ✦ ✦

Time splits. I am nowhere. And then...

I snap upright in my body. The hunter has gathered me in his arms and is holding my head up.

Shola convulses on the grass.

She sits up, something feral in her gaze.

I reach for her and fall into the same void I felt with the hunter; her familiar warmth, now an empty shell — a dead end. I lose track of time and wake up on the grass, sun rays glinting through leafy branches.

Her body lifts. First a tremor, then a pull, as if something beneath her has let go.

Her feet leave the ground.

She rises into the branches — too smooth, too wrong.

I scramble after her, looking upward and crashing through brush.

I lose her behind thick leaves. Shapes flicker between the trees — her outline, then not. I lunge after each one. Branches whip my face. I trip and roll onto my back, shuddering.

The hunter vanishes.

I saved a stranger and lost my wife in the same breath.

I drag myself to my hands and knees.

Trees lean inward, branches knitting a narrow corridor, a strong wind blowing with the sibilance of voices.

You have lost your way, Anjorin.

Shola hovers at the end of the corridor.

I need to save you from yourself.

I scream.

A storm of crows rises. The corridor vanishes. So does whatever held me.

My legs drag numb below the knee. Each step sends a dull shock through my spine.

The wind pries leaves apart.

Then nothing.

Light returns in fragments — the sway of bodies, the sense of being carried.

I lie among falling leaves — then arms lift me.

The smell of damp earth clings to me. Not memory — presence.

It sits in my throat, thick as rot.

Two men lower me to the ground and tip a calabash to my lips. Water spills; I swallow what I can. Hands raise my head, careful, almost gentle.

My breathing slows, lengthens. My guard loosens. I settle into stillness — and wait.

The forest does not leave. It presses close behind my eyes. Branches creak where there are no trees.

A hut replaces it. Smoke-blackened walls breathe in and out of focus. Faces gather above me — dark, indistinct — rising and receding.

For a moment, their mouths open without sound.

I swallow, cough, swallow again.

The haze thins. But the smell of wet root stays.

✦ ✦ ✦

Strangers form a loose circle.

My throat burns. "What chiefdom is this?"

"Odàrà," someone says.

Relief leaks from me.

"Please, notify the Ọ̀runṣọlá Clan that Anjorin the amoye has arrived — as promised."

Heads nod. Someone leaves to inform my hosts.

I need them now more than they need me.

✦ ✦ ✦

Four men lift me by the wrists and ankles and put me on a stretcher. Folded into a contraption for carrying an antelope's carcass, I sway and tilt as they carry me into the sunset.

They smell of sweat and iron, of damp cloth, of something faintly medicinal ground into their skin. Each step jolts me; it finds my injured leg without fail, a blunt pulse that blooms, fades, returns, keeping time with their stride.

Behind us, voices call — wavering, breaking, gathering again, never reaching me.

We pass through the village in half-light. Murmurs trail us, low and constant, like flies. Children peer from doorways, eyes wide, fingers hooked into wood. Elders pause mid-task — hands hovering above baskets, tools stilled — their faces set in grave calm.

They carry me to a hamlet where Tade waits with her kin in the courtyard. Relief breaks across her face, like a crack through stone. She steps forward and welcomes me without question. Hands guide me down. I close my eyes as they set me on the ground and surrender to the stillness that follows.

A dark, elderly man steps forward. "I am the Baale of this clan. I welcome you."

Hands guide me down. I close my eyes as they lower me to the ground.

"Welcome, Mindpiercer."

"Thank you. My wife should be here. An egbere took her."

The Baale's face crinkles more. "There are too many of them here, stealing travelers, taking their speech — their direction — and wiping their memories."

The younger man beside him leans forward. "By morning, she may not know her own name. We must move at dawn." He studies me. "Finding her is easy. Driving the egbere out is another matter."

"I would like to join the search."

"What do you mean?"

"She may not return unchanged."

"I will not leave her."

Tade, her mother, the Baalẹ, and two uncles file out.

Later, footsteps return. A tray rests beside me: amala and ewedu. I swallow a few bites and turn on my side.

I close my eyes.

✦ ✦ ✦

The forest returns.

The moment before Shola tries to wake me.

Stop. Don't touch me. An egbere is inside me.

She does not hear.

I should have killed it when I had the chance.

✦ ✦ ✦

Light presses against my eyelids.

I open them.

Darkness fills the room, broken by a lantern. Its flame wavers too close, heat grazing my skin. Early morning — or the hour before it claims the night. I recall the men who carried me.

The oil smokes, thickening the air with a bitter scent. Shadows of two men stretch along the walls. A chill in my chest. A cold knot behind my sternum. Small. Quiet. I press my fingers to it, rubbing. Nothing. No warmth, no pulse, no pain.

I told myself it was nothing.

"We're leaving to look for your wife. You can stay."

"No. I'm coming." My voice scrapes as I rise. My joints protest, rippling.

✦ ✦ ✦

Outside, five figures wait.

The sky hangs low, drained gray over mud walls and packed earth. The ground cool, damp. Night clings to corners.

I step forward. Every gaze weighs me.

They give their names. "Ranti," says the only woman among them.

When they finish, Gbanko speaks. I recognize him from last night. He was the one who stood by the Baale and talked about leaving at dawn. A looped rope moves in his hands like a prayer bead. The others grip cutlasses.

"What are those for?"

"For clearing brush," Sheyi says.

✦ ✦ ✦

In the forest, we step beneath canopies darker than the sky. They close in, sealing off the last of the light.

Gbanko pauses. His hand lifts — not to halt us, but to measure something above. "Watch the branches. They glide — before they learn to move like us."

His gaze lingers upward a beat too long. The others follow it, not trusting the ground to hold what they already know.

We fall into a single file, moving with the steadiness of a run before a foray. No scanning of the undergrowth, no second looks. The path matters less than the end of it.

Unease settles in me.

I sense their resentment. It comes in restrained currents.

"Why not check for footprints? There should be a trail."

Gbanko slows, but not enough to break from the rest. He glances at me. "You're the Mindpiercer, aren't you?"

"That's what some call me."

A flicker of a smile crosses Sheyi's mouth. The others shift, small adjustments of shoulders and breath.

"You're here to grant Ọ̀runṣọlá a painless death, they say."

"I am."

The air seems to draw inward.

Sheyi shuffles closer, curious.

"You're an amoye, aren't you? Tell me — do you know what he did to me as a child?"

Her voice is even, almost careful. The words land without tremor.

"I don't judge. I simply leave them lighter."

Something slips out of her, a sound that might have been a laugh.

She steps closer and stops inches from me. Heat rises from her skin. "You defy the gods by easing their punishment? Or do you think you know justice better than they do?"

The forest recedes.

"You think easing his pain changes what's coming to him?"

"Let him suffer. And die."

I turn to Gbanko.

Tension gathers. His jaw locks. His shoulders rise. "He violated my brother for years. My brother killed himself. Is that justice?" His gaze drifts into the trees.

They form a loose crescent around me — Gbanko to my left; Sheyi too close on my right; Ranti, and the other two, behind.

"Seize him."

Hands grab my arms. My cutlass is gone before I feel it leave. A knee drives into my back. I drop forward.

The ground hits my cheek.

I cry out as they tie me.

Rope bites into my wrists — tight, deliberate, the knots pulled with more care than haste.

"What is this?" I ask.

Gbanko spits.

"I want to find my wife," I yell.

A palm cracks across my face. My head snaps sideways.

"Your wife met her fate of the forsaken — of those unprotected by the gods." He pares down his words, anything more could slip into something he can't control.

Ranti stands behind me. The uncertainty that once clung to her is gone; in its place — something deliberate.

Gbanko's shifts drag at him.

His grief hasn't softened over time like corn in salt water. It sits in him, a scar tissue thickening.

Sheyi stands closest. Too close. His anger comes in waves. Beneath it is a sharp, restless vigilance. He watches everything — my hands, my breath, the space between us — as if waiting for something to slip.

My breath falters. "I'm no judge."

Ranti laughs. The sound is thin.

"You are lost."

I flinch from the pressure of her words — from her need to believe it.

The woman is not a child anymore. She is not trying to be whole. She learned how to carry emotional weight without letting it show. Until now.

Now, she must correct a silence she can no longer bear.

"Be quiet, demoniac," one of the two others shouts. "Your madness ends today."

"Hold him still."

A foot drives into my spine. Pain flares.

Sheyi presses down harder.

I could break them if I chose.

If I don't do it now, it will be too late.

I breathe in. Hold it.

Their hands press harder. Rope bites deeper.

My chest tightens.

A flicker —

Feathers between my fingers.

The cockerel's pulse against my palm. Wild. Desperate. Alive.

I loosen my grip —

Laughter.

Not here. Not now.

But it comes anyway.

Their faces blur into the past — open mouths, pointing fingers —

He cannot kill.

The rope tightens.

A knee digs into my spine.

My breath stutters.

If I let them decide —

If I fold again —

It ends.

The blade rises. Waiting.

For me to decide.

I am what they awaken.

I recoil — not from death.

From becoming that boy.

The cringing coward is still here.

Or is he?

The Pleasurepain slips loose, weaves behind them.

Their grief. Their heat. If I could take away their anger, I could loosen their hands...

A thread slips into Ranti...

She stiffens.

Into Gbanko.

A fist slams into my jaw.

The world snaps back.

"Finish him. He's doing something," Gbanko says.

The blow lands. Then another.

Hands force my head down, my neck exposed.

Their certainty. Their right to decide what I am.

Restraint is... thin.

Worn.

How many times have I held back? For people like this?

A blade rises.

"Make it quick."

Something in me ripples in pattern.

Not rage.

Not fear.

Void.

Pain strips you down. What remains cannot fend off despair. Go ahead and fault me for wanting to spare a soul; the gods will exalt me in this world and the next.

My essence coils — then spears outward.

It pierces Ranti first.

Then Gbanko.

It spreads through all five, linking them —

one body, a shared pain.

They convulse. Eyes roll white.

Their bodies jerk against the link — five throats stunned to silence. Each body rattles as the anguish of the slaughtered hollows them out.

My grip tightens.

I feel where to stop.

But I don't.

My ribs stiffen.

No flow of air.

I struggle for breath.

They convulse at my feet. I wait for something to twist inside me.

Nothing comes.

A fly lands on the bridge of my nose.

Can't lift my hand to brush it away.

I release them, and they collapse to the ground.

The ringing returns.

Insects.

Wind.

Silence.

I remain on my knees.

Ranti lies twisted, face emptied.

Gbanko's eyes stare without sight.

The others lie broken.

I stand over their stillness, legs firm.

No nausea.

No cringe.

My stomach doesn't turn.

I look at my hands. They are steady.

No tremor. No recoil.

I wait.

My hands stay steady.

Since my encounter with the egbere, I've blanked out a couple of times.

With the cockerel, I fought harder not to kill.

I stand over my search party, refusing to look at their pain-sculpted faces.

The forest waits.

Vultures will descend on this spot and so will curious passers. Unless... Unless I mask their smell with leaves and incense.

Shifting soil and cutting fronds, I cover them, one by one.

By the time I stand, the sun has crossed the sky.

I burn the prayer incense in my pouch.

My eyes don't twitch with tears.

And I don't make a request to the gods on their behalf.

Putting out sparks of sentience — even those as small as bugs — used to destroy me.

The deed is done. Embrace the dark.

I crouch over the mound of leaves and earth that hides five bodies.

Five lives. Gone.

By my hand.

Done with my gift — a gift meant for discernment.

Crows scream overhead.

I follow.

Palms slip against grit and damp leaves. I push myself forward. My legs struggle to remember their purpose. The ground tilts, sways.

I retrace my path by instinct. The forest presses close — branches snagging at my clothes, roots rising like knotted bones — then loosens.

Space opens.

The forest thins.

Crows scream overhead.

I follow their flight.

Mud walls appear between trees. A goat bleats. A child laughs.

Human sounds.

I step into Odàrà as if I never left it — as if I do not have blood on my hands.

✦ ✦ ✦

Word has spread through the village about a possessed woman who was found near a creek. I follow the murmurs to the center of the large gathering, a circle that keeps its distance.

At the center, Shola — bound to a stake driven deep into the earth.

Her presence unsettles. Egberes hold no power beyond the deep forest, yet no one risks it.

She snarls. Saliva threads from bared teeth. Her body jerks against the rope, wrists and ankles raw. The sound that tears from her throat carries no language. Her eyes blaze — unmoored — skidding past faces, searching.

My stomach drops.

This is not my wife.

"Is this your wife?" the Baalẹ asks.

"Yes. Thank you."

"Where is your party?"

"I don't know. I injured my foot and they went ahead."

The Baalẹ studies me too long.

They still need the Mindpiercer.

I ask to be taken to Ọ̀runṣọlá's solitude so I can finish what I came to do. Urgency prickles across my skin. I need to leave before they uncover what lies beneath leaves and loam.

✦ ✦ ✦

Sunlight stands at its peak when we stop at a small tent at the end of a narrow path. A human consciousness pulses inside — faint, fading. The air carries bitterness.

Tade and her mother pull cloaks over their faces, murmur prayers, and slip inside.

When they emerge, their cheeks shine with tears.

I turn to the Baalẹ. "Shall I proceed?"

"Will you not pray for his soul?"

I consider the tent. "That is not my work."

They call a priest. He mutters a hurried prayer from a distance, words tumbling, fear thinning his voice. He does not step closer.

When he finishes, I close my eyes and open myself.

Pain floods in.

Heat surges beneath my skin. My temples throb. My lower back locks tight. Strength drains. My limbs fill with ash. A gust slips through my clothes — I shudder, teeth rattling.

My stomach lurches. I drop to my knees and vomit, bile burning my throat.

I sever the link.

The fever vanishes. The pressure lifts. I take his sickness, then release it. His pain remains only as a memory.

"Are you ill?" the Baale asks.

"I am fine now," I say, wiping my mouth. "I traded my health for his sickness."

"Thank you," Tade's mother whispers.

"It is time. To send him on."

They nod.

I guide the Pleasurepain into the fading consciousness, sharpening and twisting until the consciousness clings and spasms in agony.

It slips free.

My sixth kill. In a close span.

"It is done," I shout to the crowd.

They set the tent ablaze. Flames climb. Cloth snaps. Smoke rises straight into the sky.

Some weep.

Others leave.

—

The circle around me widens.

—

The distance grows.

The priest leaves without looking at me.

An old woman spits into the dust.

The wind shifts. Ash from the burning tent drifts toward me.

No one speaks to me.

Tade's mother invites me to join them for lunch, but I decline.

Can't please everyone. The least I can do is embrace those who embrace me, but time is against me. If my search party is found, questions will be raised — and so will my neck, with the help of a rope.

✦ ✦ ✦

Late afternoon. I prepare to leave. Shola thrashes as they strap her belly-down across a donkey, limbs hanging on either side. The animal stamps, uneasy.

"Be careful in the forest. Egberes are strongest there. Strong enough to take your body. But the gods protect you."

I lead the donkey away.

Their eyes follow, feeling sorry for me.

✦ ✦ ✦

The forest exhales fumes of damp soil and manure.

On a lonely footpath, I gaze at what remains of my wife.

My last words to her cut with pride.

Now I walk beside a body that is not hers.

I stretch and seize the egbere through the Pleasurepain.

Shola screams — a sound too large for a human throat. She convulses, face twisting, foam gathering at her lips.

I force smallpox into it — fever, pain, weakness.

It resists.

Her body writhes. The Pleasurepain recoils.

She slumps.

What now?

If I swap our states, I risk trapping myself in her body — leaving the egbere in mine.

Fear presses in.

My vision blurs.

No.

She is not lost.

The egbere must leave.

The thought forms, grows.

Before I consider self-sacrifice...

Seek Moloju — the most powerful oracle in the land, a claircognizant — who lives alone on Ilẹ̀ Ìmọ̀, the steep forest hills far removed from the restless pull of souls. If anyone can come up with a solution, it's him.
